Information

📍 Is the neighborhood I'm staying in safe at night? →

General advice: Keep jewelry and expensive watches for more private venues instead of walking around with them.

Historic Center: After 9:00 PM, stay around Largo Cruzeiro de São Francisco, where you'll find restaurants, live music and more people.

Barra: Avoid Morro do Cristo late at night.

🏛️ Which museum should I visit? →

MUNCAB — a strong choice for Afro-Brazilian culture, history and contemporary Black art.

City of Music Museum — a great introduction to Salvador's musical identity. It is closed on Mondays, and admission is free on Wednesdays.

For Portuguese students and curious travelers

Bahian slang you may hear in Salvador

Short expressions that help you understand everyday conversations, humor and local rhythm.

Oxente

An expression of surprise, disbelief or emphasis. Similar to “wow,” “really?” or “what?”

Example: “Oxente, you are already leaving?”

Ó paí, ó

Literally close to “look at that.” It is used to call attention to something happening nearby.

Example: “Ó paí, ó! The band is starting.”

Castelar

To notice, understand or realize something, depending on the context.

Example: “Did you castelar what he meant?”

Lá ele

A humorous response used to reject an ambiguous or suggestive phrase and distance yourself from it.

Example: Someone says something with a double meaning, and the response is: “Lá ele!”
